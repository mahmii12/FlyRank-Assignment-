Which pages decline first, before a reviewer ever opens them?

A ranked scoring model for content review, built and validated on the FlyRank search warehouse — 142,816 content items, 90 days of signal, and one honest question: does a learned model actually beat a human's gut-feel ranking?

01 — Abstract

Question: out of tens of thousands of content pages, which ones should a reviewer with limited time look at first? Method: a Random Forest classifier was trained on 90-day search-performance features (prior impressions, position volatility, query concentration) engineered directly from the full FlyRank search warehouse (~79M daily rows) via DuckDB, validated with a client-grouped split to test generalization to unseen clients. Result: the model reached a Precision@50 of 1.000 against a naive impressions-ranked baseline of 0.540, and its accuracy advantage over the base rate held up almost identically under both random and per-client validation (~0.165 uplift either way). Framing: this is decision-support, not causal proof — the label is a proxy for observed decline, not a verified outcome of any review action. Output: a four-category ranked action list (refresh, monitor, protect, prune) that a reviewer can act on directly.

02 — Introduction

The decision this supports

A content reviewer at scale never has time to look at every page. Given hundreds of thousands of pages across a portfolio, the real question isn't "is this page declining" in the abstract — it's which few pages deserve attention this week, out of everything that could plausibly qualify.

Two kinds of mistakes matter here, and they don't cost the same. A false positive — flagging a page that's actually fine — wastes a reviewer's limited time. A false negative — missing a genuinely high-value page in real decline — lets real traffic loss go unnoticed. Because both matter, a useful model needs to be sharp at the top of its ranking, not just accurate on average across every page.

This is why the task is framed as ranking, not classification. A reviewer works down an ordered list; they don't receive one binary flag per page in isolation. The right evaluation metric follows from that: precision within the top of the ranking, not overall accuracy.

03 — Data

The warehouse, not the sample

The full pseudonymized FlyRank internship warehouse release was used, hosted as Parquet on Hugging Face and queried directly via DuckDB — no full download, only the columns and partitions each query touched.

78.8Mrows in the daily content-performance fact table
104clients in the release (unbalanced history depth)
519,606distinct content items in the dimension table

Tables used: dim_clients, dim_content, fact_content_daily_performance (daily impressions, clicks, position), and fact_content_query_90d (query-level concentration signals). A 90-day trailing window was used per content item, split into a prior 45-day period (features) and a most-recent 45-day period (label).

After requiring a minimum prior-period impression volume (≥50) and dropping rows with missing feature values, 142,816 content items qualified for the window, narrowing to 116,676 rows after feature completeness checks.

Excluded and why: client, content, and query identifiers are pre-anonymized hashes in the released dataset itself — no raw client names, domains, or URLs appear anywhere in this paper, the underlying notebooks, or the repo. No credentials or private query text are referenced.
04 — Methodology

Label, features, and the leakage check that mattered

Label definition (a proxy, stated plainly)

A content item is labeled declining if its most-recent 45-day impressions fell below 80% of its prior 45-day impressions: is_declining = imp_last45 < 0.8 × imp_prev45. This is a threshold-based proxy for "worth reviewing," not an observed outcome of any actual review or refresh action — no such outcome exists in this dataset.

Features

All features are computed from the prior 45-day window and query-mix signals only — never from the window the label is drawn from, which was the key leakage check applied throughout: imp_prev45, pos_volatility (standard deviation of average search position), visible_queries, rare_share, anon_share, and top_query_share.

Validation design

Two splits were compared deliberately, changing nothing else between them: an ordinary random 75/25 split, and a GroupShuffleSplit by client_hash_id, which guarantees no client appears in both train and test. The grouped split is the stricter, more honest test — it asks whether the model generalizes to clients it has never seen, not just to unseen rows from clients it already knows.

Precision@50 — the metric that matches the decision

Of the top 50 pages each approach ranks highest, what fraction are genuinely declining? This is the metric that matches how a reviewer actually works — down a short list, not across the whole dataset.

Checked against a false-positive artifact: the top-50 predicted probabilities cluster near 0.995, not tied at an identical 1.0, and correspond to 50 distinct content items — consistent with genuine model confidence rather than a duplication or leakage bug.

Does it generalize to clients it has never seen?

Raw accuracy isn't directly comparable across the two splits, since each has a different base rate. What matters is uplift over the base rate in each case:

Random split — accuracy 0.781, base rate 0.617+0.164
Grouped split (unseen clients) — accuracy 0.835, base rate 0.669+0.166

The uplift is nearly identical either way — evidence the model's advantage isn't an artifact of clients overlapping between training and test. Validated across 13 distinct clients in the held-out grouped set.

06 — Limitations & honest framing

What this can and cannot claim

Can claim: which pages show statistical evidence of decline, based on observed historical search signals — impressions, position, query concentration — and that a learned ranking outperforms a naive impressions-based ranking on this dataset.
Cannot claim: that refreshing a flagged page causes recovery. No controlled experiment (e.g. refreshed vs. non-refreshed pages, A/B tested) exists in this dataset — that would be required to support a causal claim.
Cannot claim: any reverse-engineering of a search engine's ranking algorithm. The model learns patterns in FlyRank's own observed performance data, nothing about how any search engine itself operates internally.

Two further honest caveats: a meaningful share of top-ranked recommendations receive tied, near-maximum confidence scores, meaning a secondary tiebreaker (e.g. raw impression volume, content age) would be needed for a strict top-N cutoff in production. And the grouped validation, while a genuinely stricter test than a random split, covers only 13 distinct clients in its held-out set — a real but modest sample for a claim about cross-client generalization.
